National tenants are the anchor occupiers within a Woodfine professional centre. The term refers specifically to tenants in the academic, medical, and civic categories whose institutional identity — the university, the hospital network, the government agency — carries regional recognition and whose presence establishes a building's professional character. In the [[mix-of-use|mix-of-use]] model, national tenants perform the same function for a professional building that a warehouse club or home-improvement superstore performs at the [[power-centres|power centre]] level: they generate credibility and destination traffic that attracts the remaining occupier categories.

## Why national tenants, not just stable tenants

Institutional occupiers in the academic, medical, and civic categories are not merely stable — they are nationally or provincially recognized entities whose presence in a building communicates institutional validation. A regional hospital's outpatient clinic, a university's satellite campus, or a provincial court services office signals to prospective business and laboratory tenants that the building operates at an institutional standard. This signalling effect is what the term "national tenant" captures: it is about identity and market credibility, not just lease duration.

Business and laboratory tenants — the categories that complete the [[mix-of-use|mix-of-use]] composition — make location decisions partly on the professional environment signalled by co-occupants. A building occupied by a regional health authority and a university faculty is a more credible environment for a professional services firm or a clinical research operation than a building whose tenancy is purely commercial.

## Tenant categories as anchor types

**Academic** tenants as national tenants are institutions with provincial or national accreditation: faculties, colleges, training centres, and research institutes whose identity is established by an academic governance structure external to the building. Their occupancy is governed by institutional decision-making cycles that extend beyond standard commercial lease terms.

**Civic** tenants as national tenants are publicly funded organizations — municipal, provincial, or federal government offices, Crown corporations, regulatory bodies — whose presence reflects a public mandate. Civic occupiers do not select office space based on commercial real estate market conditions; their site decisions are governed by operational requirements, public service delivery mandates, and procurement frameworks.

**Medical** tenants as national tenants are licensed health practitioners and institutions regulated at the provincial or national level: physician practices, specialty clinics, diagnostic labs, and hospital outpatient facilities. Medical regulatory licensing ties practitioners to specific jurisdictions and practice settings, producing structural occupancy persistence.

## Relationship to building income stability

National tenants are the primary income anchors in the Woodfine proforma model. Their lease terms, institutional creditworthiness, and low relocation probability provide the stable income foundation against which the remaining [[mix-of-use|tenant mix]] is layered. A building with confirmed national tenant commitments in at least two of the three anchor categories — academic, civic, medical — has demonstrated demand in the highest-stability occupier segments before the business and laboratory categories are fully leased.

This income composition is a structural feature of the direct-hold thesis rather than a coincidental tenant mix outcome. Site selection, building design, and leasing strategy are all oriented toward establishing national tenant anchors before the building reaches stabilized occupancy.
