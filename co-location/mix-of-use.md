Mix-of-use is the tenant curation model for Woodfine professional centres. It designates five categories of professional occupier — academic, business, civic, laboratory, and medical — whose combined tenancy produces a stable, self-reinforcing professional ecosystem within a single building. The model is structurally distinct from generic mixed-use development, which combines residential, commercial, and retail in a single building. Mix-of-use combines only professional categories, optimizing for occupier stability and cross-category demand reinforcement.

## The five tenant categories

**Academic** tenants include educational institutions, research units, student services, and academic administrative functions. Academic occupation is institutionally anchored: lease decisions are made by institutional governance rather than individual entrepreneurs, and occupation is tied to program accreditation and institutional mandate rather than market performance cycles.

**Business** tenants include general corporate, administrative, and professional services offices. Regional business tenants — serving the local economy rather than a national corporate headquarters function — exhibit demand persistence tied to local economic activity rather than national corporate consolidation cycles.

**Civic** tenants include government offices, regulatory agencies, publicly funded service organizations, and Crown-affiliated entities. Civic occupation is the most structurally stable category: lease terms are subject to public procurement processes, occupier continuity is legally mandated, and demand is independent of private-sector business cycles.

**Laboratory** tenants include scientific research, clinical testing, specialized manufacturing quality control, and academic lab extensions. Laboratory fit-outs are capital-intensive and purpose-configured; the switching cost of relocation is structurally high, producing longer effective tenancies than standard office occupiers.

**Medical** tenants include clinical practitioners, specialty clinics, diagnostic services, and medical administrative offices. Medical occupation is demand-anchored by regional population health needs, regulatory licensing that ties practitioners to specific jurisdictions, and capital-intensive equipment installations that create high relocation friction.

## Why the five-category structure

Each of the five categories exhibits demand characteristics that are largely independent of the others: a medical practice's renewal decision is not correlated with a business tenant's lease termination. The mix-of-use model assembles occupiers whose demand drivers are structurally uncorrelated, reducing the probability that a lease event in one category propagates into building-level vacancy.

This is different from a diversified tenant base achieved through multiple tenants within the same category. A building with five different law firms is diversified within the business category but exposed to common-mode shocks affecting the legal sector. A building with academic, civic, and medical tenants in addition to business tenants has demand drivers that do not share a common cyclical factor.

## Distinction from mixed use

The term "mixed use" is not used in the Woodfine portfolio framework. Mixed use in urban planning refers to the vertical or horizontal integration of residential, retail, and commercial functions — a design principle driven by urban density and pedestrian activation goals. Mix-of-use is a tenancy composition strategy, not a land-use designation, and it operates within a building exclusively dedicated to professional occupiers.

## Relationship to the national tenant anchor

[[national-tenants|National tenants]] — the academic, medical, and civic occupiers whose institutional identity carries market recognition within the regional professional community — serve as the anchor component of the mix-of-use model. Their presence validates the building as a professional destination, making it more attractive to the business and laboratory categories that complete the mix.

The co-location framework positions the professional centre beside national retail anchors. Within the building, the mix-of-use framework positions national professional tenants as the demand anchors for the remaining commercial space. The co-location strategy and the mix-of-use model operate at different scales — site and building — but both use anchor credibility as the mechanism for attracting the broader occupier demand the portfolio requires.
