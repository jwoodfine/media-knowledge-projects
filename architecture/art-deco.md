Art Deco is an architectural and decorative style that emerged in Europe in the early twentieth century and reached its peak expression in the 1920s and 1930s, particularly in commercial architecture across North American cities. The style takes its name from the 1925 Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes held in Paris, though the distinctive characteristics that define it — geometric ornament, stepped or setback massing, symmetrical composition, and lavish use of new industrial materials including reinforced concrete, aluminium, and plate glass — had already appeared in European architecture in the decade before the exhibition.

The movement emerged from a confluence of design reform efforts in Paris and Vienna at the turn of the twentieth century. The Société des artistes décorateurs (Society of Decorative Artists), founded in 1901, elevated decorative artists to the same professional status as painters and sculptors. The Vienna Secession, formed in 1897, produced architects including Josef Hoffmann, whose Stoclet Palace in Brussels (1905–1911) is considered a prototype of the Art Deco style: geometric volumes, symmetrical composition, reinforced concrete structure covered with marble plaques, and finely-sculpted ornament. New materials and technologies — particularly reinforced concrete, which had been used only for industrial structures until Auguste Perret began applying it to apartment buildings in Paris in 1903 — gave architects the freedom to create larger, more complex forms.

## Formal characteristics

Art Deco commercial buildings are identifiable by a consistent set of formal characteristics that distinguish them from both their historicist predecessors and their modernist successors. Stepped or setback massing — the building's profile narrowing toward the upper floors in a series of horizontal terraces — is the defining volumetric quality. This was in part a response to North American setback requirements (New York's 1916 Zoning Resolution required building faces to step back from the street as they rose), which Art Deco architects turned from a regulatory constraint into a compositional opportunity.

Ornament in Art Deco is geometric rather than organic: chevrons, sunbursts, stylized floral forms, eagles, and abstract representations of speed and energy, all executed in low relief rather than the deep carving of earlier classical ornament. These motifs were applied to façades in bronze, terracotta, glazed ceramic tile, and highly polished stone — materials that were novel at the time and that now distinguish Art Deco buildings as a premium category of historic commercial stock.

The interior spaces of commercial Art Deco buildings — bank lobbies, department store atria, office building entrance halls — were characterised by elaborate ceiling treatments, decorative metalwork in lift enclosures and grilles, and polished stone or terrazzo floor surfaces. These interiors were intended to project financial stability and institutional permanence to tenants and customers.

## Commercial building legacy in secondary markets

Art Deco commercial architecture arrived in North American secondary cities in the late 1920s and 1930s, carried by the expansion of national retail and banking networks. Bank branches, built by chartered banks at significant capital investment to signal permanence and creditworthiness, were among the most consistently Art Deco building types in regional cities. Department stores, hotel towers, municipal buildings, and professional office blocks of the same era share the stepped massing and geometric ornament that identify the style.

In most North American secondary cities — cities with populations between 50,000 and 500,000 — the downtown commercial fabric of the 1920s and 1930s represents the first purpose-built commercial construction of the modern era. These buildings replaced earlier frame construction, wooden storefronts, and modest brick commercial blocks from the nineteenth century. They were financed by local and national banking capital at a time when secondary markets were growing faster than primary markets, and they were built to institutional standards intended to last a century or more. Many have done exactly that.

The heritage status of Art Deco commercial buildings in secondary market downtowns is a material factor in co-location site evaluation. Buildings from this era are frequently subject to municipal heritage designation, which limits demolition and imposes conditions on exterior alteration. The designation provides certainty — a heritage-designated building will not be demolished for competing development — but may limit the intensification or conversion options that would make a site viable as a Woodfine Professional Centre or adjacent-to-Power-Centre node.

## Heritage context and co-location evaluation

Art Deco commercial precincts in secondary market downtowns establish the character of the established commercial node. The concentration of pre-war commercial buildings along a main commercial street signals market longevity — tenants have operated continuously in the area for 80 to 100 years — and typically correlates with high pedestrian traffic, established retail patterns, and public transit access. These are the indicators that co-location site evaluation identifies as markers of node quality.

The challenge for co-location in Art Deco heritage precincts is parking. Buildings of this era were designed for pedestrian access, with minimal or no surface parking. The parking ratios that professional service tenants require — three stalls per 1,000 square feet of leasable area — are rarely achievable on heritage commercial streets without structured parking. Co-location at or near Art Deco commercial precincts therefore depends on the availability of structured parking adjacent to the heritage zone, or on transit patterns that reduce the automobile-access requirement for the target tenant mix.

The most effective co-location nodes adjacent to Art Deco commercial precincts are typically on the secondary streets and peripheral parcels that surround the heritage core — sites that benefit from the pedestrian traffic and commercial activity of the heritage node without the heritage overlay on the individual site. This is consistent with the Woodfine siting thesis: proximity to an existing Power Centre or established commercial node is the site-selection criterion, not direct address on the heritage block.

Art Deco architecture is followed in the North American commercial building chronology by the modernist buildings of the 1950s and 1960s, which replaced much of the pre-war commercial stock in primary markets but left many secondary market Art Deco buildings intact. The survival rate of Art Deco commercial buildings is higher in secondary markets than in primary markets, which is one factor that makes secondary market downtowns visually distinctive and commercially differentiated from the standardised suburban commercial corridors that surround them.
