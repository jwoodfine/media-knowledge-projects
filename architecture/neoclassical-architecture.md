Neoclassical architecture is the architectural style produced by the Neoclassical movement that began in the mid-18th century in Italy and France and spread throughout the Western world. The style was a deliberate return to the forms and principles of ancient Greece and Rome — not the Renaissance and Baroque revivals that had already drawn on classical antiquity, but a more archaeologically rigorous and doctrinally austere version of classicism that aimed to strip away the excesses of Late Baroque ornament and return to what its practitioners understood as a purer and more authentic classical style.

The movement was enabled by the development of archaeology as a systematic discipline and by the publication of accurate records of surviving classical buildings. The excavations at Pompeii and Herculaneum (from 1748) brought intact examples of Roman domestic and civic architecture to European awareness. The Grand Tour — the standard educational journey of wealthy young English aristocrats through France and Italy — produced generations of patrons and architects who had seen classical ruins in person. The writings of the German art historian Johann Joachim Winckelmann, published in the 1750s and 1760s, provided an influential theoretical framework for understanding Greek art and architecture as superior in its simplicity and nobility to the Baroque decoration that had immediately preceded it.

In formal terms, neoclassical architecture is defined by its emphasis on the wall surface rather than carved relief; by symmetry and regular geometric composition; and by the application of classical orders — columns, pilasters, entablatures, and pediments derived from Greek Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian precedents — as organizing elements of the façade. This is distinguished from Baroque classicism, which had used the same vocabulary but treated it dynamically, with curving walls, dramatic contrasts of light and shadow, and elaborate sculptural ornament. Neoclassicism simplified, flattened, and geometricised.

## Palladianism and the first wave

The earliest phase of the neoclassical reaction in Britain and Ireland built on the Palladian architecture of the early 18th century. Palladianism was a return to the principles of the 16th-century Venetian architect Andrea Palladio, whose *I quattro libri dell'architettura* (The Four Books of Architecture, 1570) had described the principles of Roman temple architecture and applied them to domestic and civic buildings. Colen Campbell's *Vitruvius Britannicus* (1715), which documented British buildings inspired by Palladio and Vitruvius, and the *Designs of Inigo Jones* (1727) established the Palladian vocabulary in 18th-century Britain.

The great Palladian houses of England — Holkham Hall in Norfolk, Chiswick House, Stourhead — brought the unornamented classical vocabulary into the mainstream of aristocratic building patronage. The same tradition produced the first generation of neoclassical public buildings in Britain and Ireland: court houses, custom houses, town halls, and institutional buildings designed with columned porticos, symmetrical facades, and plainly treated wall surfaces.

## The Greek Revival and North America

From about 1800, a second wave of neoclassicism — the Greek Revival — drew on more recently available knowledge of ancient Greek architecture. The Society of Dilettanti's 1751 expedition to Greece, led by James Stuart and Nicholas Revett, produced the first accurate measured drawings of Greek temples. Their publication *The Antiquities of Athens* (from 1762) gave European architects access to Greek Doric and Ionic forms for the first time. The Greek Revival became the dominant idiom for public buildings in Britain, Continental Europe, and North America in the early 19th century.

In North America, the Greek Revival defined the first generation of purpose-built institutional architecture in secondary markets. Bank buildings, custom houses, court houses, post offices, and market buildings throughout the United States and Canada from 1800 to 1860 were built in the Greek Revival mode: a columned portico or pilastered façade, a symmetrical main elevation, and stone or brick construction signalling permanence and institutional gravity. The Second Bank of the United States in Philadelphia (1818–1824), designed by William Strickland, is a canonical example. In Canada, the Nova Scotia Legislature Building in Halifax (1819) uses the same vocabulary to signal governmental permanence in a secondary market city. Thomas Jefferson applied neoclassical principles at the University of Virginia in Charlottesville (1826), with the Rotunda at its centre.

These buildings were instruments of institutional communication. A chartered bank choosing the Greek temple form for its branch building was making a claim: that it had the permanence, authority, and trustworthiness of the institutions of antiquity. A court house using the same vocabulary asserted the legitimacy of the state. The association of neoclassical form with institutional credibility was so durable that it persisted through the 19th century and well into the 20th, producing neoclassical bank buildings in North American secondary cities that postdate the formal transition to modernism.

## Heritage significance in secondary markets

The neoclassical commercial and institutional buildings of North American secondary markets — primarily from the period 1800 to 1880 — are the oldest purpose-built structures in most secondary city downtowns. They predate Art Deco commercial buildings by 50 to 100 years, and they are typically the most architecturally prominent buildings on the main commercial street: the tallest, the most ornate, and the most solidly constructed. Heritage designation is nearly universal for neoclassical civic and institutional buildings. Many have been listed on national heritage registers and are subject to both federal and municipal heritage protection.

The economic significance of neoclassical heritage buildings in co-location site evaluation is largely indirect. These buildings are rarely available for redevelopment — their heritage status, combined with their institutional ownership (court houses are owned by governments; historic bank buildings are frequently now owned by cultural institutions or converted to restaurant and hospitality uses) removes them from the private development market. Their significance is rather as markers of node quality: a secondary market downtown that retains its neoclassical institutional core has demonstrated 200 years of continuous commercial activity, which is the single most reliable predictor of sustained professional-service tenant demand.

The neoclassical institutional building also serves as a spatial anchor for the commercial fabric of the surrounding blocks. Court houses generate pedestrian traffic from legal professionals, court staff, and members of the public attending hearings. Historic bank buildings converted to cultural uses generate foot traffic at different hours. The professional service demand that these institutional uses generate — lawyers, accountants, notaries, insurance agents, medical and dental practices — is precisely the tenant profile that Woodfine Professional Centres are designed to serve. Proximity to a secondary market neoclassical institutional core is therefore a positive signal in co-location site evaluation, and sites within walking distance of this core benefit from the established professional-service ecosystem that surrounds it.
